Community Schools Initiative

A cornerstone of Mayor Jim Kenney’s vision for education in Philadelphia is to establish 20 community schools by 2023.  Community Schools are public neighborhood schools that also serve as community centers, improving access to resources and supports for students, families, and the neighborhood.  Each school offers a unique set of services and programs based on feedback from the community.  By connecting schools with city support and public-private partnerships, these community hubs will strengthen neighborhoods, and improve living and learning conditions for children and their families.  A City-employed Community School Coordinator manages the process at each school while working closely with the principal, community members, and service providers.  In the first two cohorts in 2016 and 2017, the Mayor’s Office of Education (MOE) designated twelve schools across the city as community schools.

 

The first step for community schools is to assess the needs of the school and surrounding community.  MOE utilized a variety of tools to gather feedback from the school community about what needs were most important, including interviews, focus groups, and surveys.  In addition, MOE analyzed community datasets including information from the American Community Survey, Public Health Management Corporation's Household Health Survey, the Community Health Assessment, and this tool - the Stress Index.  The Stress Index maps were presented to each school to help understand the context of the neighborhood when identifying community school priorities.  These collective assessments culminate in the creation of Community School Plans for each school that describe the next steps in fulfilling neighborhood needs.

 

In addition to the needs assessment, community schools use data to inform decision-making in other ways.  For example, the Community Schools Progress Report tracks the early planning and implementation of the initiative, and assesses progress against national best practices.  The report tracks progress of the initiative at two levels: 1) central staff and 2) the first cohort of nine Community Schools announced in the summer of 2016.  Each level of assessment contains the elements, benchmarks, and indicators that characterize the launch of a successful community school according to best practices and research conducted on similar efforts.

 

Lastly, Research for Action (RFA) has been selected to serve as a third-party, independent evaluator of the Community Schools initiative beginning with the 2017-2018 school year.  RFA will utilize a suite of indicators to assess the quality of implementation in the first cohort of community schools.  RFA is tracking and analyzing quality implementation and short-term outcomes in community schools, which includes participation in programming/services and satisfaction with the services provided.
